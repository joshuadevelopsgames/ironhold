# Master Builders Compass — who to study, and what each one teaches

**Status:** v0 — synthesized from training-data familiarity; not yet image-validated
**Provenance:** claude-proposal (a few entries cross-reference creators already credited in `_sources.csv`)
**Use:** read once, refer back when designing a new structure to pick a "lineage" to learn from

The Minecraft architecture scene has matured to the point where named
builders own recognizable styles. Studying *who* built *what* — and what
they consistently optimize for — is faster than re-deriving the same
lessons from raw screenshots.

This compass is opinionated. It does not try to be a comprehensive list
of every famous Minecraft YouTuber. It picks the builders whose work has
the most to teach a generator that is trying to produce structures that
do not look procedural.

---

## How to read this file

Each entry has the same shape:

- **Builder / corpus** — name, with a one-line identifier
- **Signature** — the visual / structural choices you can recognize them by
- **Single biggest lesson** — the one thing they teach better than anyone
- **When to copy from them** — the structure type where their lessons apply hardest
- **Limit** — what their style does NOT do well, so you know when to look elsewhere

When in doubt about a build, ask: "Whose style is this aiming at?" — then
read that entry and apply the lesson.

## I. Vernacular medieval houses (cottages, taverns, villages)

### Swordself / SWORDSELF_MC

- **Signature:** dramatic asymmetric gables, twin brick chimneys, dark slate
  roofs, bay windows protruding from second floor, diamond-lattice windows.
  Builds always feel "lived in," never sterile.
- **Lesson:** *the silhouette tells the whole story.* Their houses are
  recognizable from a quarter-mile away because every roofline is broken
  by chimneys, dormers, or stacked gables. There is no flat-topped wall.
- **Copy when:** building any peasant house, town house, cottage, tavern.
- **Limit:** signature is too cozy for fortresses or sacred spaces.
- *See:* SWORDSELF entries (r00, r02, r06, r07, r08, r15, r16, r17) in `_sources.csv`.

### Stevler

- **Signature:** half-timber Fachwerk discipline. Restrained palettes —
  often just one wood + one stone + one accent. Highly photogenic close-up.
- **Lesson:** *fewer materials, used with rigor, beats many materials
  used loosely.* Half-timber works only because the diagonals and verticals
  follow a consistent grammar across the wall.
- **Copy when:** building German / Alsatian / Tudor-style domestic.
- **Limit:** discipline can read as repetitive at large scale.

### ChronoComet

- **Signature:** external wall chimneys, stone-base + wood-upper, white-tinted
  stained glass, integrated cottage gardens.
- **Lesson:** *the chimney is a facade element, not just a roof element.*
  Letting the chimney run up the outside wall (rather than through the
  roof interior) creates a strong vertical line that anchors the house.
- **Copy when:** building cold-climate cottages, modest stand-alone houses.
- **Limit:** not for monumental scale.

### CraftingMatthew

- **Signature:** central stair tower with conical cap rising from a domestic
  house, dark slate roofs, multiple brick chimneys, hayrick by the door.
- **Lesson:** *one vertical element makes an otherwise-flat house monumental.*
  A single skinny tower mid-house tells the reader "this person matters."
- **Copy when:** building the most prominent house in a village, a mayor's
  residence, an innkeeper's tower.
- **Limit:** overused, it becomes the "every house has a tower" parody.

### Abfielder

- **Signature:** cantilevered jetty second floors, mixed window language
  (some arched, some square), stone-wood transition trim. Builds tend to
  be downloadable schematic-shaped — clean enough to extract and reuse.
- **Lesson:** *the cantilever (jetty) is the cheapest way to add a strong
  horizontal shadow line to a two-storey building.* It also implies wealth
  (only a real carpenter could brace that overhang in the real world).
- **Copy when:** building taverns, two-storey town houses, anything Tudor.
- **Limit:** the jetty implies an urban-density grid — looks odd on a
  rural single house with empty land around it.

### ImpetusBuilds

- **Signature:** fantasy castles with conical tower caps, foliage integration,
  multi-tier silhouettes, cliff-base contour landscaping.
- **Lesson:** *the castle and the cliff are one build.* Most amateur castle
  builds plop a square keep on flat ground. Impetus pushes the structure
  into the terrain — half-buried roots, retaining walls, exposed bedrock
  beneath the keep.
- **Copy when:** building any structure on uneven ground.
- **Limit:** style assumes dramatic terrain — wasted on a flat plain.

## II. Megabases, tasteful palettes, and texture-block discipline

### GeminiTay

- **Signature:** restrained pastel palettes, heavy texture-block variation
  (5–8 variants per "material"), natural-looking biome integration, very
  high decoration density inside, ruthlessly clean exterior shapes.
- **Lesson:** *a clean exterior shape can carry an extremely busy interior.*
  Don't try to do both. Pick which side of the wall does the noise.
- **Copy when:** the build is going to be admired from inside (a home, a
  studio, a great hall the player will spend time in).
- **Limit:** her exteriors can be too gentle for menacing structures.

### fWhip

- **Signature:** warm cozy palettes (oranges, deep reds, dark wood), heavy
  use of trapdoors as decorative trim, mansard-style roofs.
- **Lesson:** *trapdoors are the secret trim element of medieval MC.* They
  add 1-pixel-deep shadow lines that real architecture has via wooden battens
  and corbels. Use them as window shutters, wall trim courses, awnings.
- **Copy when:** anything that needs to feel inhabited, warm, welcoming.
- **Limit:** trapdoor abuse becomes its own cliche.

### Grian

- **Signature:** playful builds, recognizable motifs (the "Grian roof" — a
  steep central gable with two cross-gables forming a + plan), heavy
  variation-on-a-theme rather than novelty.
- **Lesson:** *pick one weird shape and commit to it across every roof
  on the build.* Don't try to make each roof unique. Make them all share
  the same eccentric move.
- **Copy when:** doing a village or estate where multiple structures
  must read as belonging to one designer's hand.
- **Limit:** the eccentric move depends on Grian's specific taste —
  copy the principle, not the literal shape.

## III. Castles, fortresses, gothic architecture

### Raaamseeel (BLOCK-TRUTHED — dramatic dark fortress)

- **Signature:** *vast vertical dark fortress on a colored-wool podium*.
  153 blocks tall. The litematic ships with its own 30-block-deep red-wool
  cliff at the base (~50% of the build's volume), with a snowy mountain
  biome built on top of it, and the fortress proper above that.
  ALL FOUR ERAS of stone present at 20–30% each (founding 29%, curtain
  28%, refinement 20%, modern 22% — no single block exceeds 7% of the
  wall fabric). Green wool dominates the roof zone (copper-patina look).
  Red glazed terracotta concentrated at the spire tip. A vertical color
  narrative: red base → gray-black walls → green roof → red apex.
- **Lesson:** *ship the terrain inside the litematic.* A castle that
  arrives with its own cliff is a complete artifact, not a building
  needing a setting. Also: *the egalitarian 4-era palette mix* —
  all eras at low concentration reads as more ancient than a primary +
  accents. Also: *vertical color narrative* — make the build tell a story
  bottom-to-top through color shifts, not just materials.
- **Copy when:** building a dramatic dark fortress, evil keep, mage
  citadel, anything that needs to be visually striking from afar.
- **Limit:** the wool podium technique requires the user to swap red_wool
  → red_terracotta or red_concrete after pasting (wool is flammable);
  document this in the share notes.
- *See:* full block-by-block analysis in
  [../reference_builds/raaamseeel_dark_fortress_study.md](../reference_builds/raaamseeel_dark_fortress_study.md).
  Source litematic preserved at `structures/external/raaamseeel_dark_fortress.litematic`.

### Onctaaf (BLOCK-TRUTHED — first confirmed reference)

- **Signature:** "natural-stone medieval castle on a terrain plinth."
  Light gray palette (andesite + smooth_stone + stone_bricks, ~0%
  deepslate), heavy slab-as-trim, moss + cracked stone in equal measure,
  torch-and-lantern lighting (no soul fire), trapdoors as wall battens.
  No portcullis — defense is the moat + the hillside.
- **Lesson:** *the "default" master-castle palette is NOT dark.* Andesite
  is a primary wall material, not an accent. Slabs are the dominant
  trim (~10x stair use). The castle includes its own dirt + grass +
  moss + water terrain inside the litematic bounding box.
- **Copy when:** building a peace-time castle, royal seat, stately
  manor, ancestral keep — anything that isn't a defensive fortress
  under active siege.
- **Limit:** style does NOT fit dark/evil castles, gothic cathedrals,
  or active fortresses (no portcullis, no machicolation).
- *See:* full block-by-block analysis in
  [../reference_builds/onctaaf_castle_study.md](../reference_builds/onctaaf_castle_study.md)
  and palette in [../palettes/natural_stone_medieval.md](../palettes/natural_stone_medieval.md).
  Source litematic preserved at `structures/external/onctaaf_castle.litematic`.

### Westeroscraft (community, not solo)

- **Signature:** the single best public corpus for grand-scale medieval
  Minecraft. Castles modeled on real-world medieval fortifications
  (Edinburgh, Caernarfon, Krak des Chevaliers) translated to the GoT
  fictional world. Massive scale (a small castle is 150×150 blocks),
  extreme material storytelling, downloadable schematics with a published
  style guide.
- **Lesson:** *real-world fortifications have already solved the problem
  you're solving.* Look up the layout of an actual castle the build wants
  to evoke — gatehouse, ward, keep, chapel, kitchens, garderobe — before
  placing a single block. The whole-castle plan dictates every wall.
- **Copy when:** any structure larger than a single building. Especially
  castle / fort / fortified manor.
- **Limit:** their scale and texture density assume long build times.
  A procedural generator can't match it. The lessons that translate are:
  plan-first, material-storytelling, room-program rigor.

### Smitey

- **Signature:** gothic cathedrals at enormous scale. Pointed arches with
  multi-block tracery, flying buttresses, rose windows, stained-glass
  programs that tell a single coherent visual story across an entire
  building.
- **Lesson:** *pointed arches are a system, not an ornament.* Once you
  commit to gothic, every opening — window, door, niche, gable peak —
  must be a pointed arch in proportion. Mixing pointed and rounded reads
  as "I didn't know which I wanted."
- **Copy when:** chapels, cathedrals, gothic-faction castles.
- **Limit:** gothic doesn't fit early-medieval or peasant builds. Don't
  mix it with cottages — it reads as parody.

### Adamantis

- **Signature:** cathedrals and ecclesiastical interiors. Long compositional
  axes ending in a single focal element (altar, throne, rose window). Use
  of light as content rather than as utility.
- **Lesson:** *every great religious space has one focal point, and the
  whole building's geometry funnels toward it.* The axis from the door,
  down the nave, to the altar is the only line that matters. Everything
  else (side aisles, transepts, niches) is in service of pointing your
  eye to that axis.
- **Copy when:** chapels, throne rooms, cathedrals, any room where one
  thing must dominate.
- **Limit:** great-axis design fights against rooms that need to feel
  egalitarian (mess halls, taverns).

### BlueBits

- **Signature:** organized castle builds. Strong silhouettes, clean
  curtain walls, gatehouses with character, varied tower types.
- **Lesson:** *give each tower a job.* The corner tower with a flag is
  the lord's quarters; the squat tower with a wide base is the dungeon;
  the tall slim one is the watch tower. When every tower is distinguishable,
  the castle reads as functional rather than decorative.
- **Copy when:** any castle with multiple towers.
- **Limit:** assumes the player will see all sides — a castle on a cliff
  with only one visible face doesn't need this much variety.

### DigsRTU / WiederDude / GoCreative

- **Signature:** castle tutorial-style builds. Crenellations executed with
  rigor, gatehouses with portcullis + murder hole + drawbridge, machicolations
  on towers. Tend to favor "this is how a medieval castle would actually
  work" over "this is what looks cool."
- **Lesson:** *the defensive details are not just decoration — they imply
  a working castle.* A castle without a barbican, a portcullis, or arrow
  loops reads as a stage set.
- **Copy when:** any castle that wants to feel like a real fortress.
- **Limit:** technical-realism can drift into being a museum diagram.

## IV. Cross-cutting lessons distilled from all the above

These are the lessons that appear so consistently across master builders
that they're effectively universal. Each one is a candidate for promotion
to a standalone principles document if it isn't one already.

### 1. Plan the silhouette first

Every master builder's work has a memorable silhouette before you read
any texture. Sketch the silhouette before placing blocks. If the silhouette
is a rectangle, the build will fail no matter how much texturing you do.

### 2. Three motifs, repeated mercilessly

Already codified in [design_language.md](design_language.md). All masters
follow this. If the build has 7 different window shapes, it isn't the
result of inspiration — it's the result of indecision.

### 3. Material storytelling carries history

Already codified in [material_storytelling.md](material_storytelling.md).
All masters use 2+ "eras" of stone. The lord didn't build the whole
castle in one day; the build should reflect that.

### 4. Light is content, not utility

Already codified in [lighting_dramaturgy.md](lighting_dramaturgy.md).
Master builders use *less* light than tutorial builders. Dark corners are
content. A great hall lit by one chandelier reads as moody and inhabited;
the same hall covered in lanterns reads as a hardware store.

### 5. Decoration density tracks function

Already codified in [decoration_density.md](decoration_density.md). The
chapel has the highest density in a castle. The undercroft has the lowest.
Equal density everywhere reads as procedurally generated.

### 6. Edge decoration is non-optional

Already codified in `community_batch1_24_patterns.md` (P22, P23, P24).
Every master decorates corners, doors, and roof edges. Flat walls are
where bad builds live.

### 7. The cantilever / overhang is the cheapest "wealthy" move

A second floor that overhangs the first (jetty) implies skilled carpentry.
A roof that overhangs the wall (eave) implies a real builder, not a
serf piling rocks. Most amateur builds skip these because they're
"not load-bearing in Minecraft" — that's exactly why they read as elite.

### 8. Trapdoors are the secret trim

Used as: window shutters (open or closed), awnings above doors, decorative
wall battens, roof-edge trim, chimney sleeves, lectern accessories,
table aprons. fWhip-style.

### 9. Always integrate with terrain

Master builders never plop on flat ground. They cut into hillsides,
extend foundations down to bedrock, route walls around boulders. A perfectly
flat foundation is a tell that a generator built it.

### 10. Verticality matters more than footprint

A 5×5×30 watch tower reads as more architecturally serious than a 20×20×8
keep. Master builders push height before width. The vertical eye-line is
what makes a build photograph well.

## V. Servers and corpora worth ingesting in batches

When the catalog needs a major expansion, these are the highest-yield
sources:

| Corpus | What you'll learn | Scale |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| **Westeroscraft** (westeroscraft.com) | grand-scale castle programming, real-world fortification mapping | enormous, downloadable |
| **Planet Minecraft** ("medieval" filter, top all-time) | cross-section of styles, density of variety | huge, variable quality |
| **r/MinecraftBuilds** (top-of-all-time, medieval-tagged) | what voters value (already partially ingested in `community_batch1_24_patterns.md`) | curated by upvotes |
| **schemat.io** (medieval / castle filter) | downloadable schematics for analysis with `schem_to_isc.py` | downloadable, variable license |
| **Conquest Reforged showcase** | extreme texture-density, ignore the modded blocks and study composition | downloadable but mod-specific |
| **YouTube tutorials by Smitey, Adamantis, BlueBits, GoCreative, JINTUBE, WiederDude** | step-by-step rationale for each move, not just final shots | watchable but slow |

## VI. The 30-second "whose style?" cheat sheet

When generating a new structure, decide in 30 seconds which lineage to pull from:

| If the structure is... | Look at... |
|------------------------|-----------|
| a peasant house | Swordself, Stevler, ChronoComet |
| a tavern or innkeeper's house | Abfielder, Swordself (r17), fWhip |
| a stand-alone watchtower | ImpetusBuilds, r10, r14 references |
| a small village (multiple buildings) | Linard, the SWORDSELF series collectively |
| a chapel | Smitey (small scale), Adamantis (interior axis) |
| a cathedral | Adamantis, Smitey |
| a small castle / fortified manor | BlueBits, DigsRTU |
| a stately castle / peace-time noble seat | **Onctaaf** (block-truthed) — see [natural_stone_medieval.md](../palettes/natural_stone_medieval.md) |
| a grand castle / royal seat | Westeroscraft + Smitey for the chapel |
| a "dark castle" / evil keep | **Raaamseeel** (block-truthed) — see [raaamseeel_dark_fortress_study.md](../reference_builds/raaamseeel_dark_fortress_study.md) + [castles_grand_study.md](../reference_builds/castles_grand_study.md) |
| a mage tower / wizard's lair | ImpetusBuilds (silhouette) + Smitey (interior) |
